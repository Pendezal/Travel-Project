
# How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating “user stories” help with this? 

For me, interpreting user needs is not just about reading requirements, it’s about thinking deeper about what the user is actually trying to do. I noticed that when I only focused on what was written, I missed the bigger picture. User stories helped me slow down and think in terms of purpose instead of just features. For example, the “Top Five Destination List” started off as a simple display feature, but I realized the user’s real goal was decision-making. That shift changed how I approached development. User stories made it easier to connect the technical work back to real user intentions instead of just completing tasks.

# How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work? 

I approach development as a process of building, questioning, and adjusting. One thing I learned is that trying to figure everything out on my own slows me down more than it helps. Agile showed me the value of breaking work into smaller pieces and improving over time instead of aiming for a perfect solution from the start. Practices like sprint planning and retrospectives helped me stay organized, but more importantly, they helped me reflect and improve. Moving forward, I want to keep using Agile practices, especially focusing on iteration and being more comfortable with adapting as things change.

# What does it mean to be a good team member in software development? 

Being a good team member means being clear, honest, and willing to ask questions even when something feels small. One of the biggest things I learned is that staying quiet or assuming something leads to more problems later. When I started asking more direct questions, everything became easier to understand and work through. It also means recognizing that every role is connected. The Product Owner, tester, and developer all depend on each other, so communication is what keeps everything aligned. To me, a strong team member is someone who helps create that clarity, not just someone who completes their own tasks.
